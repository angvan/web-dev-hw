How To Identify a Venomous Rattlesnake

Snakes · Head Shape · Eyes and Pit · Belly Scales · Color Patterning · Behavior · Baby Rattlesnakes · First Aid · Resources

Snakes

If you live in Montana, or any other area that is home to venomous snakes, it behooves you to know how to differentiate between those and the non-venomous varieties. Montana has ten different native snakes, and only one of those is venomous. It is known as the Prairie Rattlesnake. Although you would do well to to stay away from all snakes in the wild, knowing how to tell the difference between one that can hurt you and one with a mostly benign bite is beneficial since not all snakes are of danger to you.

The following information will help you to identify an adult rattlesnake. After the adult identification you will find a small section about baby rattlesnakes, which can be a little more challenging to identify.

Head Shape

The first thing to look at when identifying a rattlesnake is the head shape. A non-venomous snake will have a more rounded head, whereas a venomous rattlesnake will have a triangular shaped head.

(Back to Top)

Eyes and Pit

The next clue for indentification is the shape of the eyes and whether or not there is a "pit" present. You would have to be pretty close to the snake to determine this characteristic, but it is worth noting, although it is not recommended that you put yourself in harm's way to investigate. A "pit" is a hole half-way between the nose and the eye. Venomous snakes have this "pit" characteristic and have an elliptical pupil much like a cat's eye. A non-venomous snake has a round pupil.

(Back to Top)

Belly Scale Patterning

The third means of identification has its limits in usefulness since you would need to be holding the snake in order to see the belly scales. Also, some snakes may violate this pattern. However, if you found a dead snake and were interested in seeing if the snake followed this rule of identification, you would look at the scales underneath the end of the tale.

Please note: It is not recommended to pick up a dead rattlesnake as even dead snakes can perform "reflex biting" when handled.

(Back to Top)

Color Patterning

Color patterning is another method of identification but is not always accurate either as some snakes share similar patterns between breeds. Most Prairie Rattlesnakes have black or dark blotches extending along the back to the tail where they change from blotches to rings. Just like with scales, this characteristic could be missing. Another rattlesnake characteristic is that they are fatter in the middle and taper towards their tail and head. The most consistent method of identification will always be the head and eye shapes, and the presence of a "pit".

Behavior

Of course, we couldn't identify a rattlesnake without mentioning its tell-tale rattle located at the end of its tail. Rattlesnakes will hold their tails vertically so that their rattle can be heard. Keep in mind, however, some rattlesnakes lose their rattle, and other non-venomous snakes will shake their tails from side to side and strike objects to make a rattle sound. Mimicking a rattlesnake is their attempt to frighten off would be predators.

Baby Rattlesnakes

Baby rattlesnakes pose their own threat. The head shape and "pit" on the face are also a distinguishing feature in baby rattlesnakes, and they have the distinctive patterning that is shown in most adult rattlesnakes. But be aware, baby rattlesnakes do not have rattles, rather they possess a small knob or button at the end of their tail. As the baby grows and sheds its skin, it will grow its rattle bit by bit. Although baby rattlesnakes do not have as much venom as adults, their venom is more potent, and baby rattlesnakes cannot control the amount of venom they secrete into their victim making them potentially more dangerous than the adult. Adults not only can control the amount of venom secreted, sometimes they don't secrete any venom when they bite unlike the baby who will secrete venom with every bite.

First Aid for Venomous Snake Bites

  • Get away from the snake to avoid additional bites!
  • Do not attempt to capture the snake, even if it's dead.
  • Identify the snake or take a picture if you can do it safely.
  • Take off all restrictive items as venom can cause swelling.
  • Never cut the wound.
  • Do not use your mouth to suck out the venom.
  • Keep calm as a raised heart rate can cause the venom to travel quicker through your body.
  • Obtain assistance as soon as possible.
